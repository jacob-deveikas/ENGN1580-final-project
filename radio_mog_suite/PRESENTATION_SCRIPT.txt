SHORT PRESENTATION SCRIPT

1. Channel first
We measured the speaker-mic path directly with a swept audio waveform. The response is not flat, so the modulation choice absolutely depends on the channel. The plot shows where the room/laptop pair is strong, weak, and notched.

2. Modulations
For the graded uncoded system we prepared FSK, QPSK/4-QAM, and 64-chip temporal CDMA.
FSK uses noncoherent tone-energy comparison.
QPSK maps two bits per symbol to four constellation points, pulse shapes with an RRC filter, matched-filters at the receiver, and estimates timing and phase from a known sync sequence.
CDMA spreads each 100 bps bit over a 64-chip PN waveform on a 12.8 kHz carrier and detects by correlation.

3. Timing recovery
All modes use a chirp preamble for coarse packet acquisition. FSK then refines symbol timing by searching the known sync sequence. QPSK matched-filters then searches symbol phase over the known QPSK sync. CDMA aligns by maximizing the known sync correlation.

4. Carrier recovery for QPSK
The QPSK receiver downconverts by the measured carrier, then uses the known sync symbols to estimate a phase offset and phase ramp. The constellation plot shows the corrected received signal points.

5. Data rate and P_e
The graded demos send deterministic uncoded PRBS bits. The receiver regenerates the same PRBS from the shared seed and computes P_e directly. The running P_e loop accumulates bit errors over repeated packets.

6. Interference resistance and adaptation
We scan ambient power in the candidate FSK bands. If a narrowband interferer contaminates a band, we switch to the quieter band or transmit both low and high profiles for diversity. The interference generator lets us demonstrate this with a sine jammer or white noise.
